# Product

## Register

product

## Users

GameRec is for players with growing game libraries or backlogs who struggle to decide what to play next. They want a way to find new games, organize their play order, track mood and goals through journaling and session logging, and understand their own taste through ratings, reviews, and play history.

The primary user wants recommendations that feel more personal than popularity lists. They are trying to turn scattered interest, backlog pressure, and vague taste signals into a queue that feels intentional and worth playing.

## Product Purpose

GameRec is a personal game discovery and backlog management app. It helps users browse games, maintain a library, plan a play queue, rate and reflect on completed sessions, and receive AI-assisted recommendations grounded in what they have played, rated, reviewed, or journaled about.

Success means the user can quickly answer: what should I play next, why does it fit me, what have I enjoyed before, and how do I want to shape my future play time?

## Brand Personality

Cinematic, thoughtful, composed.

Cinematic because games should feel like premium media worth browsing, not just database entries. Thoughtful because recommendations should feel personal and taste-aware rather than generic. Composed because the interface should feel calm, professional, and organized instead of loud, gamified, or cluttered.

## Anti-references

GameRec should not feel like a generic SaaS analytics dashboard, a Steam clone, a neon-heavy gamer launcher, an overdesigned AI product, or a spreadsheet-style backlog tracker.

It should avoid looking too corporate, too gamer-bro, too AI-gimmicky, or too database-like. The experience should not overwhelm users with charts, tables, badges, or glowing effects.

Reference direction:

- IMDb for cinematic, credible, professionally organized media listing and detail pages, without copying IMDb directly.
- Letterboxd for taste, ratings, reviews, and personal media identity.
- Plex for cinematic library presentation, large artwork, and media-first browsing.
- Backloggd for game-specific tracking, but GameRec should feel more polished and less community-database-like.
- Playnite for library management ideas, while avoiding a desktop launcher feeling.
- Steam only lightly for ownership and library expectations, never as a visual clone.

## Design Principles

1. Make games feel desirable and memorable. Screens should foreground cover art, screenshots, metadata, and taste signals in a way that treats games as premium media.
2. Explain taste without turning the app into analytics software. Recommendations, ratings, and journal signals should be understandable, but charts and metrics should stay restrained.
3. Keep planning calm and structured. Queue management, backlog browsing, and session history should reduce decision pressure instead of creating more inventory work.
4. Let personality come from the user's play history. The interface should feel personal through saved games, ratings, notes, moods, and recommendations, not decorative gimmicks.
5. Stay composed under complexity. Dense information is acceptable when it is scannable, layered, and easy to browse.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA. Core flows should be keyboard-friendly, reduced-motion preferences should be respected, and visual states should not rely on hue alone. The interface should preserve readable contrast across dark surfaces, artwork-heavy layouts, badges, recommendation explanations, and compact metadata.
